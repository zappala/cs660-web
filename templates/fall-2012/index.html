Syllabus

This graduate course explores advanced topics in computer networks, focusing on fundamental research being conducted to improve the Internet. The course covers a set of classic research papers from a broad spectrum of topics, mixed with some of the latest research in these areas. Topics include congestion control, routing, Internet measurements, social networking, and wireless networking.

The prerequisite for this course is CS 460 Computer Communications and Networking or its equivalent.

Learning Objectives

At the end of the course, students should be able to:

  • understand a broad set of Internet research topics and the open research issues in the area,
  • have some expertise in the methods used to conduct Internet research,
  • the ability to conduct independent and original research, including formulating research questions, studying related work, formulating solutions, collecting and analyzing data, and presenting research results.

Organization

We will study one research area every two weeks. For the first week, we will all read and discuss the same papers. For the second week, each student will select and read a different paper and then we will discuss these in class. The papers chosen should be the most important in the area. By the end of the two weeks, each student should be familiar with the area.

Materials

Links to research papers will be available on the class web site. Additional papers can be found in the IEEE and ACM digital libraries, which are available free from on campus.

For the labs you may need reference material on various programming languages, depending on your prior background and the type of project you do. Regardless of the project, you will be most successful if you use a scripting language to process your data and generate plots. Here are the Python and C++ books that I recommend:

  • Dive Into Python
  • How to Think Like a Computer Scientist: Learning with Python
  • Practical C++ Programming, 2nd Edition, by Steve Oualline, O'Reilly & Associates, December 2002, ISBN 0-596-00419-2.

Assignments

We will maintain a shared Wiki for the class that will cover the general topic of Internet research. Each student will be responsible for writing a one-paragraph summary of a paper for each area we cover. In addition, one student will be assigned to collate these summaries to write the page for this area, discussing the area in general, including the summariesof the papers we have read, and discussing open research problems. To give everyone a chance to write two area summaries, we will split areas into sub-areas as needed.

Labs will consist of exercises that will help you to gain familiarity with some of the tools available to the research community. Labs will include using ns3 simulator and PlanetLab .

The final project will be a research proposal that addresses an open question in the area of networking. The report will take the format of a CS Master's Thesis proposal, describing why the problem is important, discussing related work, and outlining a research plan to solve the problem and validate the solution.

Grading

Grading will be based on an scale of 0 to 100, with standard letter grades assigned. Your final grade will be computed by weighting the assignments as follows:

Paper Summaries and Area Summary30%
Labs30%
Research Project40%

Group Work

Students may work in groups of two or three for the labs. Each student must still complete their individual Wiki and entries and research proposal. Each student in a group is expected to participate fully in all activities of every lab.

Late Policy

All work must be turned in on time -- turn in partial work if you are not done. Medical or other exceptions must be given prior to the due date.

Educational Policies

Honor Code Standards

In keeping with the principles of the BYU Honor Code , students are expected to be honest in all of their academic work. Academic honesty means, most fundamentally, that any work you present as your own must in fact be your own work and not that of another. Violations of this principle may result in a failing grade in the course and additional disciplinary action by the university.

For this course, some assignments are categorized as group work. For these assignments, you may form a group that works together to produce one solution. Any assignment not categorized as group work must be done individually. You are encouraged to generally discuss problems with other groups or students, but you may never use some other group's or student's solution or code in any way. The use of sources (ideas, quotations, paraphrases) must be properly acknowledged and documented.
